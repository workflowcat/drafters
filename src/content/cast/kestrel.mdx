## Kestrel Health Systems, Inc.

**Kestrel** is the fastest-moving client we have and the one most likely to ruin a weekend. They operate on a time zone that seems to be "always 4pm Eastern", their Slack is always on fire, and every quarter they discover a new compliance surface that needs a new integration by next sprint. Their CTO **[[cast/lena-krivonos|Dr. Lena Krivonos]]** joined them in early 2025 from Horizon, and we inherited a working relationship with her on day one — which is the only reason the engagement functions at all.

They pay well. They pay fast. They sign things at night. They then, six weeks later, send us a Slack message asking if we can "just quickly look at" a HIPAA business-associate subcontract their legal team drafted during someone's flight. We bill for the look.

## Why they hire us

Backend work on their clinical workflow platform and the HIPAA-adjacent integrations around it. The integrations are the sharp end: Epic, Cerner, payer systems, state registries. Each one is a compliance puzzle wrapped in an undocumented REST API.

## What they push on

- **Speed over paperwork.** They'll start work on a handshake and backfill the MSA. We don't do that anymore — see **[[subcontractor-parted-midproject-2025]]** — but we've had to set and reset that boundary with them at least four times.
- **Indemnities.** Their legal team insists on broad indemnification for IP and data claims. We accept IP infringement indemnity (we always have) but push back hard on data-claim indemnities, because HIPAA liability is an uninsurable blast radius. Our line: **we will carry professional indemnity for our errors; we will not carry HIPAA breach liability for their system**.
- **Data handling.** BAA in place, yes, but they constantly want to push PHI into environments we haven't pre-approved. Every single sprint has a 10-minute "no, that dataset stays in your environment" conversation.

## What they don't push on

- **Rates.** They barely read the rate card. If we say a senior engineer is $180/hr, they say yes and add a second engineer. This is good and dangerous in equal measure.
- **Termination.** They want us on long runs. Their churn risk isn't that they'll fire us — it's that a cooler project across the office will lose them interest and slow payment will follow.
