## Nordwind Versicherung AG

**Nordwind** is the client who reminded us that in Germany, procurement isn't a department — it's a religion. Their Einkauf team has a binder. The binder has clauses. The clauses are non-negotiable in principle, negotiable in the last 48 hours of a quarter, and catalogued by clause number rather than topic. They call a liability cap "Klausel 14.3" before they call it a liability cap.

They are the client that taught us to stop trying to "educate them out of" their template and to instead learn its structure, find the seams, and propose amendments that fit inside their grammar instead of against it.

## Why they hire us

Backend work on their pensions platform: long-lived modernisation projects on a legacy core, all in their own on-prem Kubernetes footprint. No cloud, no SaaS, no cute stuff. Everything is an RFP. Everything has a response deadline measured in weeks.

## What they push on

- **Their paper, not ours.** Nordwind won't sign our MSA. They send theirs. We fought this once in 2024 and lost; since then we maintain a redline library keyed to Nordwind's template sections. See **[[new-eu-client-2026]]** for the full play-by-play.
- **Liability.** Their default cap is "1× annual fee", lower than anything we normally accept. We push back to **1.5× annual fee with the standard carveouts** — which their Einkauf grudgingly allows because "it's close to Klausel 14.3".
- **Data residency.** All code, all data, all backups, all CI runners must be in the EU. No US subprocessors without written notice and a 30-day objection window. We comply because it's become normal in our stack anyway.
- **Subcontractors.** Any subcontractor touching the project must be named, approved in writing, and covered by a flow-down DPA. This costs **[[roles/legal|Legal]]** ~3 days per subcontractor.

## What they don't push on

- **Payment terms.** 45 days, paid on day 45, every single time, by Sparkasse wire, with a 14-digit reference we must quote exactly. Once the invoice is in their system, the outcome is deterministic.
- **Rate discussions.** They prefer long, flat, predictable contracts and will accept a modest uplift at renewal without drama. What they don't tolerate is surprise.

The architect is the one who actually knows what we're building. The procurement lead is the one who decides whether we get paid.
