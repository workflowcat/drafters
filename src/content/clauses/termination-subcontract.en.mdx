**Term.** This agreement takes effect on the Start date and
continues until terminated by either party in accordance with
the terms below.

**Termination by Contractor (first 3 months).** During the
first **three (3) months** from the Start date, the
[[Contractor]] may terminate this agreement at any time by
giving the [[Subcontractor]] **1 day** prior written notice.

**Termination by Contractor (after 3 months).** After the
initial three-month period, the Contractor may terminate this
agreement at any time by giving the Subcontractor **14 days**
prior written notice.

**Termination by Subcontractor.** The Subcontractor may
terminate this agreement at any time by giving the Contractor
**14 days** prior written notice, regardless of the duration
of the agreement.

## Why 1 day in the first 3 months

This is a probation mechanism. The first quarter with a new
subcontractor is a trial period. If something goes wrong
(technical quality, communication, fit), we need to be able
to exit quickly without a long notice period that costs us
money.

After 3 months, we assume we've invested in knowledge transfer
and integration, and losing the subcontractor costs us more.
14 days is a compromise between our flexibility and their
financial stability.

## Asymmetry with the Subcontractor side

The Subcontractor **always** has 14 days notice, even in the
first month. This is deliberately asymmetric: in the first
month **we** can exit quickly, **they can't**. The argument:
probation risk lives mostly on our side.

## What this clause doesn't include

- **Termination for cause** — immediate termination for serious
  breach. Not explicitly described; legal practice assumes
  material breach allows immediate termination without notice.
- **Partial termination** — ability to terminate part of the
  scope. Absent. Termination is always total.
- **Severance** — no compensation payments on termination. The
  Subcontractor is paid only for work performed before the
  termination date.
